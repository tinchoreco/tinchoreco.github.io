The Prisoner's Dilemma as a Computer

How strategic interactions can compute anything

To be a universal computer, a system needs three things: logic gates (AND, NOT), wires (to transmit signals), and memory (to store intermediate results).

Our result demonstrates that when c₁ < 0, the prisoner's dilemma has exactly these three ingredients. The concavity of the potential pushes agents to the "corners": full cooperation (1) or full defection (0). Their behavior becomes binary, like the bits of a computer.

No designer configures these logic gates. The agents' weights emerge from decentralized payoff optimization. Computation emerges spontaneously from strategic interaction, just as prices emerge from the market. In the Hayekian tradition: computational order emerges without a planner.
