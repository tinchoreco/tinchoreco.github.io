Why Can't We Predict the Economy?

The prisoner's dilemma, patience, and cooperation

February 2026 8 min read

Imagine two countries trading with each other. Each can choose to open their markets (cooperate) or put up barriers (defect). If both open, both gain. If one opens and the other closes, the one who opened loses. If both close, both lose, but less than the one who opened alone. This is the prisoner's dilemma — the most studied structure in game theory.

Classical theory says that in a single encounter, the only rational strategy is to defect. But if countries interact repeatedly, cooperation can emerge: if I know we'll meet again, it pays to cooperate today to maintain the relationship tomorrow. This fundamental result, known as the "folk theorem," is one of the pillars of modern economics.

But the folk theorem has a problem: it assumes agents are infinitely patient, or at least that their patience is a fixed datum given from outside. Where does patience come from?

Patience Emerges from Certainty

Our research program proposes an answer: patience emerges from certainty. When an agent understands their environment well — when they can predict with reasonable precision how others will react — they're willing to sacrifice short-term gains for long-term benefits. When they don't understand their environment, when uncertainty is high, they become myopic: they take what they can now, because the future is too unpredictable to plan for.

This idea has a precise formalization. Each agent maintains Bayesian beliefs about how others will react. The variance of those beliefs determines a risk premium, which in turn determines the discount rate. When beliefs are precise (low variance), the agent discounts the future little and cooperates. When imprecise (high variance), they discount the future heavily and defect.

The Institutional Threshold

The result is that cooperation doesn't depend on a parameter given from outside, but on the institutional quality of the environment. Transparent institutions reduce uncertainty, which increases patience, which enables cooperation. Opaque institutions increase uncertainty, destroy patience, and make defection the only rational strategy.

There exists an institutional quality threshold below which cooperation is sustainable and above which it collapses.
