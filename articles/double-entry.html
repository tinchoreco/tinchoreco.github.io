The Double Entry of Energy: Thermodynamics, Information, and the Two Principles of Economics

February 2026 16 min read

Accounting has a sacred rule: for every debit there is a credit. If someone receives money, someone gives it. The total sum is always zero. This rule—double-entry bookkeeping, invented by Luca Pacioli in 1494—is not an arbitrary convention. It is a conservation principle: money is neither created nor destroyed in a transaction, only transferred from one account to another.

Physics has the same rule: it's called the first principle of thermodynamics. Energy is neither created nor destroyed, only transformed. The total sum is always zero.

The accounting double entry is the double entry of energy. And this observation, which might seem like a curiosity, turns out to be the gateway to a much deeper connection—a connection that reveals that economics has not one but two fundamental principles, exactly like thermodynamics, and that confusing the first with the second has been the central error of centuries of economic thought.

The First Principle: Conservation

Money in a transaction is zero-sum. If I buy a coffee for three dollars, I lose three dollars and the seller gains three dollars. The sum of our monetary balances hasn't changed.

This is a pure conservation principle, identical in structure to the first principle of thermodynamics. And it has an exact correlate in game theory: zero-sum games. In a zero-sum game, what one player gains is exactly what the other loses.

Mercantilism—the economic doctrine that dominated Europe from the 16th to 18th centuries—was essentially a first-principle theory. Mercantilists saw the economy as a zero-sum game between nations: the world's wealth was fixed, and one country's prosperity could only come at another's expense.

Adam Smith destroyed this vision in 1776. Wealth is not conserved, he argued. Trade is not zero-sum. Two nations can trade and both end up richer than before. The division of labor creates value that did not previously exist. Wealth is produced, not redistributed.

Smith was right. But neither he nor the economists who followed could formalize exactly what distinguishes mere transfer from genuine value creation.

The answer is the second principle.

The Second Principle: Irreversible Creation

In thermodynamics, the first principle says energy is conserved. The second says something completely different: that natural processes have a direction. Heat flows from hot to cold, never the reverse. Systems tend toward disorder. Entropy always increases in a closed system.

But the second principle also says something constructive: that it is possible to convert heat into useful work. A heat engine takes heat from a hot source, converts part into mechanical work, and expels the rest as waste heat to a cold source. The efficiency of this conversion has a maximum limit—the Carnot efficiency.

In economics, the analogous principle is: information is converted into wealth through an irreversible process whose efficiency has a fundamental limit.

The relevant information is algorithmic knowledge—the programs, technologies, production methods that firms discover and accumulate. Wealth is the output: the goods and services these programs produce. And the conversion from one to the other—production—is an irreversible thermodynamic process, governed by the second principle.

From Zero-Sum to Cooperation

The distinction between the two principles illuminates one of economics' oldest debates: is trade a zero-sum game or a positive-sum game?

The answer is: both, but at different levels.

Monetary exchange is zero-sum—first principle. When I buy bread, the money I give is exactly the money the baker receives. Pure mercantilism so far. But the complete transaction—including the good—is positive-sum. I value the bread more than the three dollars I paid (otherwise I wouldn't buy), and the baker values the three dollars more than the bread (otherwise they wouldn't sell). Both end up better off. Value has been created—second principle.

Where does this created value come from? From the same source as useful work in a heat engine: from a difference. Carnot needs a temperature difference. Trade needs a valuation difference. In both cases, the difference is exploited through an irreversible process that produces useful work (or wealth) and generates entropy.

The Error That Lasted Centuries

The central error of economics—the error that has persisted in one form or another for centuries—is confusing the first principle with the second.

The mercantilists applied the first principle to everything. Smith corrected them but couldn't formally explain why wealth isn't conserved.

Marx saw that production creates value (second principle) but tried to trace that value as if it were conserved through production stages (first principle). His labor theory of value assumes that labor "contained" in inputs is transferred to the product—a conservation law. But value is not transferred: it is created irreversibly by computation, and part is dissipated as entropy in the process.

Economics needs both principles, explicitly recognized and formally separated:

The first principle governs exchange: money is conserved, accounting balances, financial transactions are zero-sum.

The second principle governs production: wealth is created by irreversible computation, with efficiency limited by the incomputability of optimal technology.

Money is conserved. Wealth is not. And the difference between them is exactly the difference between the first and second principles of thermodynamics.
