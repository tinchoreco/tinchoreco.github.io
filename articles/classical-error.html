The Classical Error, the Thermodynamics of Value, and the Market as Self-Organization

February 2026 18 min read

Adam Smith, David Ricardo, and Karl Marx shared a profound intuition: the value of things has an objective foundation in human labor. A diamond is worth more than a glass of water not (only) because we desire it more, but because extracting, cutting, and polishing it requires enormously greater effort. Labor, the classics said, is the substance of value.

For a century, this idea dominated economic thought. And for a century, it worked reasonably well as a first approximation. But it contained a subtle error—an error the classics could not have detected because the physics of their era did not yet have the tools to formulate it. The classics assumed, without saying so explicitly, that labor-value is conserved: that the labor "contained" in iron, coal, and the worker's hours is transferred to the steel produced, like a fluid passing from one container to another.

This assumption is the economic equivalent of the first principle of thermodynamics: energy is neither created nor destroyed, only transformed. And it is incorrect for exactly the same reason that a purely energetic description of physical processes is incomplete.

The Marginalist Revolution as Overcorrection

In the 1870s, three thinkers—Carl Menger in Vienna, William Stanley Jevons in London, and Léon Walras in Lausanne—demolished the labor theory of value and replaced it with something radically different: value is subjective. It doesn't matter how much labor it cost to produce something; what matters is how much the last willing buyer desires it. Marginal utility, not incorporated labor, determines price.

The marginalists were right in what they affirmed and wrong in what they denied. They were right that demand is subjective, that use-value is ordinal and personal, that a glass of water in the desert is worth more than a diamond in a saturated jewelry store. But by rejecting all objectivity on the supply side, they threw the baby out with the bathwater.

Labor as Computation

The language that allows us to resolve this debate is the theory of computation.

In the framework I have developed under the name Turing–Walras–Chaitin, economic goods are bit sequences—digital representations of any object that can be specified, communicated, or reproduced. Production technologies are computer programs: Turing machines that transform inputs into outputs. And the objective cost of producing a good is what mathematician Charles Bennett called its logical depth: the minimum computation time required by the most efficient program to generate it.

Logical depth captures exactly what the classics were trying to formalize with "socially necessary labor time." A good is valuable on the supply side because producing it requires an irreducible computational effort.

But—and here is the crucial correction to the classics—this logical depth is not conserved.

The Second Principle, Not the First

Economic production is not like pouring water from one container to another. It's like cooking: simple ingredients are combined through a complex and irreversible process to create something qualitatively new. The value of the dish is not the "sum" of the value of the ingredients plus the cook's time. It is something that emerges from the process and cannot be additively decomposed into its components.

The classics modeled value as energy (first principle: conserved, transformed, transferred). The reality is that value behaves like entropy (second principle: created, dissipated, with an irreversible temporal direction).

This was the classical error. They were not wrong to seek an objective foundation for value in labor. They were wrong to assume that foundation obeyed a conservation law. They anticipated thermodynamics—Smith published The Wealth of Nations in 1776, Carnot formulated the second principle in 1824—and implicitly postulated a first principle of value when the correct one was a second principle.

The Duality: The Incomputable and the Subjective

Logical depth resolves the mystery on the supply side. But it has a mathematical property that changes everything: it is incomputable. No economic agent—no firm, no planner, no algorithm—can calculate the true minimum cost of producing an arbitrary good.

This generates a fascinating duality at the heart of economics:

The supply side is governed by a magnitude that is objective but unknowable. The logical depth of a good is a mathematical fact about the structure of the universe—it does not depend on opinions, beliefs, or preferences. But no agent can know it completely.

The demand side is governed by a magnitude that is subjective but knowable. The utility a consumer assigns to a good is arbitrary—it depends on their history, tastes, context—but the consumer knows it.

The equilibrium price is the point where the incomputable is reconciled with the subjective. The market is the machine that performs this reconciliation.

A New Synthesis

What emerges is a synthesis that would have satisfied both Smith and Menger, both Marx and Walras—if they had had access to computation theory and the thermodynamics of irreversible processes.

Value has an objective component (logical depth, irreducible computational work) and a subjective component (marginal utility, consumer preference). The first governs supply, the second demand. Neither the classics nor the marginalists were wrong; each had half the puzzle.

But the synthesis is not simply Marshall with new terminology. The computational formalization reveals something that classical and neoclassical economics could never see: that the objective component of value is incomputable, that production is a thermodynamically irreversible process that creates value without conserving it, and that the market is a self-organized system operating in a regime where central planning is not just difficult but logically impossible.

Value is not conserved. It is created by irreversible computation, measured by logical depth, and discovered—imperfectly but spontaneously—by a market that computes what no individual mind can calculate.
